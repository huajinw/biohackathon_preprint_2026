# 1. Introduction

## 1.0 Overall Event

Large-scale biomedical research increasingly requires integrating genomic, clinical, and imaging data across institutions. However, privacy regulations, governance policies, and ethical obligations fragment potentially transformative datasets across organizational boundaries. Federated learning has emerged as a promising path forward by enabling institutions to collaboratively train machine learning models locally and share only model parameters and aggregated statistics. This architecture allows for the preservation of data sovereignty while enabling collaborative model development, offering a path toward privacy-preserving biomedical AI applications at scale.

The Carnegie Mellon University-NVIDIA Federated Learning Hackathon for Biomedical Applications (January 7-9, 2026) convened researchers from academia, government, and industry to implement federated frameworks for disease subtyping, genetic association studies, and multimodal clinical prediction using NVIDIA FLARE. This preprint presents ten projects spanning genome-wide association analyses, histopathology harmonization, pangenome construction, ancestry deconvolution, rare disease stratification, cancer subtyping, polygenic risk score aggregation, and multimodal fusion. These proofs of principle collectively demonstrate both the versatility of federated learning for biomedical applications and the technical considerations required for successful deployment.

## 1.1 FedGen: Federated Learning Infrastructure & Synthetic Genomic Data

Large-scale genomic studies increasingly rely on multi-site collaboration to achieve sufficient statistical power for complex genetic traits, yet sharing individual-level genomic data across institutions is constrained by privacy regulations. These barriers prevent researchers from pooling the diverse cohorts needed to identify genetic variants associated with disease risk and treatment response.

Current approaches either restrict collaboration to summary statistics, sacrificing the flexibility of individual-level analysis, or require data sharing agreements that take time to negotiate and exclude potential contributors. Centralized data pooling also concentrates risk, as a breach could expose genetic data for entire study populations.

FedGen establishes the foundational infrastructure for federated genomic analysis by generating realistic synthetic datasets and deploying a server-client architecture for collaborative model training without centralizing raw data. The synthetic data layer incorporates linkage disequilibrium structure, per-site variability, covariates, and site-level imbalance to closely resemble real-world genomic properties. We validate the framework on binary phenotype prediction using logistic regression across multiple client sites with heterogeneous sample sizes and phenotype distributions. NVIDIA FLARE’s client-side privacy filters ensure that only model weight updates traverse the federation while raw genotypes remain local for each participating site.

## 1.2 FedPathHarmony: Domain Harmonization for Federated Histopathology

Integrating histopathology data across biobanks is essential for training robust diagnostic models, yet site-specific artifacts from variable staining protocols and scanner characteristics cause models to learn institutional signatures rather than biological features. This domain shift problem leads to poor generalization when models trained at one center are deployed at another, limiting the clinical utility of collaborative pathology AI.

Current federated approaches train on raw images and attempt to learn domain-invariant representations post hoc, but this strategy struggles to manifest when inter-site variations can be mistaken for disease-relevant signals.

FedPathHarmony harmonizes digital pathology images prior to federated training, explicitly reducing domain shift at the data level before model optimization starts. We demonstrate this approach on the CAMELYON17 dataset [@litjens1399HEstainedSentinel2018], which exhibits substantial H&E staining heterogeneity across five medical centers. NVIDIA FLARE's privacy filters ensure that only normalized feature representations and model updates are shared, preserving patient confidentiality while enabling cross-institutional collaboration.

## 1.3 FedViz: Auditing Federated Readiness Across Biobank Consortia

International biobank consortia have cataloged genomic and clinical data for millions of participants [@Manolio2020], yet these resources remain functionally siloed because of the harmonization required for federated training. Without understanding which variables are comparable across sites, federated models risk learning site-specific artifacts rather than disease significant biological patterns [@Patterson2006].

Current approaches to accessing data compatibility require moving data to a central location in order to compare, which is an endeavor that is restricted by privacy regulations.

FedViz provides a visualization command center for auditing harmonization gaps and quantifying federated readiness across distributed cohorts without the need for centralizing raw data. The tool is built on harmonized metadata from large-scale consortia and generates site-specific data compatibility metrics along with model performance valuations. NVIDIA FLARE’s privacy-preserving communication layer  [@Roth2022] enables FedViz: Auditing Federated Readiness Across Biobank Consortia to aggregate cross-site statistics while ensuring no individual-level data leaves institutional boundaries.

## 1.4 Omnigenome: Federated Pangenome Graph Construction: Federated Pangenome Graph Construction

Pangenome graphs that capture genetic variation across populations can unlock more accurate variant identification and improved representation of human diversity, yet current methods are limited to tens of individuals and cannot integrate data siloed across biobanks. This leaves vast amounts of high-quality long-read sequencing resources in UK Biobank and All of Us separated and unable to contribute to comprehensive population-scale references.

Currently, no existing methods enable distributed institutions to collaboratively construct unified pangenome graphs while maintaining data sovereignty, forcing researchers to choose between comprehensiveness and privacy.

Omnigenome develops federated pangemone construction using HPRC data as a proof-of-concept, alongside federated genomic hashing for APOE-locus association studies to demonstrate privacy-preserving pattern matching capabilities without exposure of raw sequences. We validate this approach on 1000 Genomes Project data for chromosome 19 GWAS focused on variants with Alzheimer's disease risk [@Wightman2021-hk; @Sabata2025Hackathon]. NVIDIA FLARE’s client-side privacy filters ensure that only graph structure updates and hashed representations traverse the federated platform.

## 1.5 Med_SNP_Deconvolution: Privacy-Preserving Ancestry Inference

Population stratification often confounds genomic analyses, with substantial allele frequency differences across ancestral groups limiting the generalizability of polygenic risk scores and association studies. Accurate ancestry inference is essential for biomedical applications, but current methods require access to individual-level genotype data [@Browning2023; @Maples2013; @Price2006; @Patterson2006], for which sharing is restricted by privacy policies.

Current dimensionality reduction and ancestry-informative marker approaches use centralized data access, creating the difficult choice between pursuing analytical rigor and obeying privacy regulations. These methods also sacrifice statistical power when applied separately within ancestry groups, and introduce harmonization challenges during post hoc analyses.

Med_SNP_Deconvolution transforms phased variant data into recombination-defined haploblock cluster identifiers that preserve population structure while obscuring individual-level variation, then integrates GPU-accelerated XGBoost [@Chen2016] and deep learning with federated infrastructure [@Roth2022]. We demonstrate high accuracy in ancestral classification on 1000 Genomes Phase 3 data [@1000Genomes2015] with interpretable feature importance scores aligned with known regions under population-specific selection. NVIDIA FLARE’s privacy filters ensure that only haploblock-derived features and model weights are communicated.

## 1.6 RAIDers: Federated Rare Disease Subtyping

Rare neurodegenerative diseases such as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) exemplify how discovery is constrained by data scarcity and fragmentation across institutions. ALS is a rapidly progressive, fatal motor neuron disease with a lifetime risk of ~1 in 350, typically leading to paralysis and respiratory failure within 2-4 years [@gonzalezsanchez2025pathophysiology; @mead2022amyotrophic; @rheenen2021common]. Each center sees relatively few patients, and ALS shows substantial clinical and genetic heterogeneity—different onset sites, progression rates, and overlap with frontotemporal dementia while most large genomic resources overrepresent European ancestries [@gonzalezsanchez2025pathophysiology; @bono2025perspectives; @mead2022amyotrophic; @anjum2025aggregation; @rheenen2021common]. As a result, individual cohorts are underpowered to map how genetic variants and ancestry-specific backgrounds shape risk and disease course [@anjum2025aggregation; @rheenen2021common; @chia2018novel].

Only about 10-20% of ALS cases are explained by highly penetrant variants in genes such as SOD1, TARDBP, and C9orf72, with the remainder reflecting complex interactions between rare and common variants and environmental factors [@arreolaaldape2025systematic; @benatar2025amyotrophic; @smukowski2022progress; @chia2018novel]. Large, cross-cohort efforts show that thousands of genomes and multi‑omics profiles are required to resolve this architecture and define molecular subtypes beyond crude severity-based groupings, yet data sharing at this scale is constrained by privacy and regulatory barriers [@bono2025perspectives; @anjum2025aggregation; @rheenen2021common].

Public resources such as ClinVar, gnomAD, OMIM, and ALS variant catalogs aggregate disease-associated variants, but they are rarely integrated into unified, mechanism‑aware models that connect pathogenic “triggers” with modifying background [@arreolaaldape2025systematic; @anjum2025aggregation; @smukowski2022progress; @dethe2025identifying]. Severity-based stratification often merges distinct mechanisms (e.g., toxic SOD1 aggregation versus TDP-43 pathology) because they lead to similar clinical outcomes, limiting therapeutic precision [@gonzalezsanchez2025pathophysiology; @mead2022amyotrophic; @huang2024variability; @dethe2025identifying].

RAIDers address these gaps in ALS by integrating 480 pathogenic variants into a federated, pathway‑specific mechanistic subtyping framework. ALS is an ideal testbed given its high unmet need, expanding but incomplete variant catalog, marked heterogeneity, and ancestrally skewed genomic data [@arreolaaldape2025systematic; @huang2024variability; @anjum2025aggregation; @benatar2025amyotrophic; @rheenen2021common; @chia2018novel]. RAIDers trains on synthetic proxy cohorts that mirror real ancestral diversity, enabling discovery of ancestry‑specific modifier patterns and pathway signatures without centralizing identifiable genomes, aligned with privacy‑by‑design approaches in neurodegeneration [@gonzalezsanchez2025pathophysiology; @quigley2025genetic; @grassano2025genetic; @chia2018novel]. Built on NVIDIA FLARE, it applies client‑side privacy filters so model training occurs locally and only aggregated parameters are shared, transforming fragmented ALS variant catalogs into clinically meaningful, ancestry‑aware subtypes while preserving patient privacy.

## 1.7 OncoLearn: Federated Cancer Subtyping with Multi-Omic Data

The biological foundations of tumorigenesis are highly heterogeneous, requiring the integration of large-scale, multi-instutional clinical and transcriptomic data to identify nuanced molecular subtypes. Accurate subtyping is important for precision oncology, yet the high dimensionality of RNA-seq data makes it difficult to train robust models on the limited, localized cohorts available to individual clinical research sites.

Current centralized training of multi-omic models is heavily restricted by patient privacy regulations and the logistical challenge of aggregating sensitive genetic datasets. Conventional frameworks struggle with non-standard molecular variations, and models trained on single-site cohorts rarely generalize well across the diverse genetic backgrounds of global populations.

To address these challenges, OncoLearn was developed as a configuration-driven deep learning platform with API and CLI interfaces for reproducible cancer genomics research. The framework is designed to support workflows in both Python and R, with configurable Docker-based development and training environments that allow experiments to be deployed across heterogeneous computing systems. OncoLearn also supports automated data acquisition from major public cancer research repositories including the cBioPortal via their REST API [@Cerami2012-ub; @Gao2013-oz; @Cerami2012-al; @Hoadley2018-hq], UCSC Xena (XenaBrowser) [@Goldman2020], and imaging datasets from The Cancer Imaging Archive (TCIA) [@Clark2013-mh; @tcia-brca]. While this work focuses on breast cancer (BRCA) as a proof of concept, the platform is designed to be easily extended to other cancer datasets and modalities.

During the biohackathon, three teams explored complementary modeling approaches within the OncoLearn framework: (1) A traditional machine learning and transformer baseline team explored transfer learning using BulkRNABert [@pmlr-v259-gelard25a] for compressing high-dimensional expression vectors into dense embeddings that were used for classification. (2) A federated learning team evaluated distributed classical machine learning using XGBoost within a privacy-preserving training setup. (3) A multimodal modeling team investigated foundation-model approaches for multi-omic integration using architectures from FM-BCMRI [@FM-BCMRI] and BiomedSciAI multi-omic models together with RNA foundation models such as biomed.rna.bert.110m.mlm.multitask.v1 [@dandala2025bmfmrnaopenframeworkbuilding].

Across these efforts, OncoLearn was used to evaluate breast cancer molecular subtype classification using TCGA data across five PAM50-like classes—Basal, Her2, LumA, LumB, and Normal—within distributed and privacy-aware machine learning workflows, where NVIDIA FLARE’s privacy-preserving filters ensure that only encoded embeddings and model weight updates are shared across the federation.

## 1.8 PRSAggregator: Federated Polygenic Risk Score Harmonization

Harmonization Polygenic risk scores (PRS) aggregate effects across thousands of variants to stratify disease risk [@lewis_2020], but most PRS are derived from European-ancestry dominant cohorts [@martin_2019] and thus transfer poorly to other populations due to differences in linkage disequilibrium and allele frequencies[@duncan_2019]. This ancestry dependence raises scientific concerns about generalizability and ethical concerns about equitable clinical deployment across diverse patient populations.

Current PRS methods require individual-level genotypes for realignment across populations, which privacy-preserving frameworks prohibit. Existing federated approaches also focus on individual-level data rather than population-level harmonization, missing opportunities to leverage the wealth of published PRS.

PRSAggregator develops a representation learning framework operating entirely at the PRS and variant level, treating each score as a client-like entity to disentangle shared versus ancestry-specific genetic architecture. We harmonize heterogenous PGS Catalog scoring files enriched with biological annotations to learn interpretable representations revealing population structure. NVIDIA FLARE’s aggregation infrastructure enables ancestry PRS harmonization across ancestries while preserving underlying variant-level contributions local to each population point.

## 1.9 MuFFLe: Multimodal Federated Fusion Learning

Integrating data across multiple data modalities (such as transcriptomics, clinical, and imaging) for tasks like cancer prognosis is essential for improving modeling power for tasks in computational medicine, but is greatly challenged by privacy regulations and clinical variations in treatment options and quality.

Current large multimodal models rely on centralized data aggregation strategies, which is infeasible due to privacy constraints such as HIPAA. While federated learning protects data privacy, existing federated learning approaches assume uniform data availability, requiring tedious pre-training data harmonization steps that can introduce bias and lead to the exclusion of sites with incomplete data.

MuFFLe (Multimodal Framework for Federated Learning) is a privacy-preserving framework that addresses these issues for cancer prediction. MuFFLe allows institutions with incomplete multimodal data to participate using zero embeddings that do not bias the model, and it simplifies data harmonization easier by introducing a way to federate embeddings for each modality, distributing the data harmonization work to individual institutions. The framework was validated on cancer outcome prediction using combined transcriptomic and clinical features. NVIDIA FLARE’s privacy-preserving communication ensures that only fused model weight updates are shared with the central server.

## 1.10 FedProFit: Federated Prediction of Deep Mutational Scanning

Predicting how mutations affect protein function is important to therapeutic development and variant interpretation, but experimental deep mutational scanning data is distributed across institutions [@Fowler2014]. Centralized training on pooled DMS datasets could improve prediction accuracy, but is often challenging to achieve due to data privacy concerns.

Current protein fitness prediction models rely on publicly available datasets and cannot leverage the private experimental data held across industry and academic institutions.

FedProFit combines pre-trained protein language models from NVIDIA BioNeMo [@BioNeMo2024] as frozen feature extractors with locally trainable prediction heads, enabling federated learning without centralization of raw data. We benchmark on ProteinGym [@Notin2023] using a four-client simulation representing distinct biological domains including human, viral, prokaryotic, and eukaryotic proteins. NVIDIA FLARE’s client-side privacy filters ensure that only prediction head gradients are communicated with a central server, which preserves privacy for sequence-function relationships at each individual institution.

# 2. Methods

## 2.1 FedGen: Federated Learning Infrastructure & Synthetic Genomic Data

### 2.1.1 Synthetic Data Generation

To ensure the Federated Learning (FL) framework implemented in this task was evaluated against representative and biologically plausible data, we generated synthetic datasets for 10 distinct sites using the LDAK software v6.1 [@speed2020ldak].

**Phenotypic Architecture:** The simulated phenotype was modeled after Parkinson’s disease (PD) case-control status. To reflect the epidemiological reality of elderly populations, we established a disease prevalence of 1%. The trait’s genetic architecture was defined on a liability scale with a SNP heritability of $h^2 = 0.25$. For each site, we designated 20 causal variants, with effect sizes determined by the LDAK-Thin model using a power parameter of $-0.25$.

**Genotypic Parameters and Site Heterogeneity:** To simulate the technical and demographic heterogeneity often encountered in multi-center studies, we introduced the following variations across the 10 sites: sample size ranged from 88,000 to 110,000 individuals per site; variant density varied between 450,000 and 520,000 SNPs per site; and Minor Allele Frequencies (MAF) were drawn from a uniform distribution between $0.01$ and $0.5$. A realistic LD structure was generated internally via LDAK to ensure the synthetic genomes maintained biological integrity.

**Data Imbalance and Flexibility:** The above simulation strategy resulted in a diverse multi-site environment characterized by varied subject counts, fluctuating SNP coverage, and distinct distributions of age and sex, providing a robust testbed for the federated models.

### 2.1.2 Federated Learning Architecture and Strategy

The FL framework for our task was implemented using a centralized server-client architecture, orchestrated via the NVIDIA FLARE (NVFlare 2.7.1) SDK as illustrated in Figure 2.1-1. This setup was designed to facilitate high-performance genomic analysis while maintaining strict data localization.
![[Preprint_Images/Team1 - FedGen/Figure 2.1-1.svg]]

**Figure 2.1-1:** **A centralized server-client architecture orchestrated via the NVIDIA FLARE** A) Illustration of FedGen’s FL infrastructure, and B) generation and distribution of synthesized genotypes and phenotypes for training local models.

**Infrastructure and Server Orchestration:** The central coordinator was deployed on an Amazon Web Services (AWS) instance. To ensure robust dependency isolation and environmental stability, the server was managed within a dedicated Python virtual environment to oversee the synchronization of federated rounds across all participating sites.

**Client Configuration and Software Environment:** On the client side, ten independent compute instances were provisioned using NVIDIA Brev GPU instances. Each site operated within a unified Docker container environment to guarantee environmental consistency. The core analytical task at each client site involved the execution of the Regenie software package v4.1 [@mbatchou2021regenie]. This was achieved by invoking the official Regenie Docker container to ensure valid whole-genome regression and association testing.

**Privacy-Preserving Design and Protocol:** The system adhered to a "privacy-by-design" principle to mitigate the risks associated with data sharing. The workflow followed a synchronous FL protocol. Only GWAS summary statistics generated by Regenie in Step 2—specifically chromosome, position, ID, reference allele, alternative allele, frequency of the alternative allele, sample size, and the test performed—were transmitted to the FL server. Raw genotypic and phenotypic data never exited the client’s secure environment.

### 2.1.3 Meta-Analysis (Model Aggregation)

To synthesize the genome-wide association study (GWAS) results from non-overlapping cohorts, we integrated two primary meta-analytical strategies into our FL framework on the server side: fixed-effects and random-effects meta-analysis. Once the FL clients completed their local GWAS, the resulting variant-level effect sizes ($\beta$) and their corresponding standard errors ($SE$) were aggregated using an inverse-variance weighted (IVW) sum. While computationally efficient, this model does not explicitly account for between-site variance.

To explicitly account for between-site heterogeneity, we also incorporated a variance component estimated through statistical tests for inconsistency. In our pipeline, this was achieved through the integration of the GWAMA software v2.2.2 [@magi2010gwama]. The server-side aggregation was embedded within a custom aggregator function designed to automatically reorganize and map the site-specific outputs to the input format required by GWAMA.

## 2.2 FedPathHarmony: Domain Harmonization for Federated Histopathology

**Patch Based Histopathology:** The CAMELYON17 dataset [@litjens1399HEstainedSentinel2018] comprises 1,300 hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)–stained sentinel lymph node whole-slide images (WSIs) from breast cancer patients. We utilized a patch-based variant of CAMELYON17, CAMELYON17-WRDS [@kohWILDSBenchmarkIntheWild2021], which comprises approximately 450,000 patches of size 96 × 96 pixels extracted from the WSIs. Each WSI was manually annotated by pathologists to delineate tumor regions, and the resulting segmentation masks were used to assign binary labels (tumor or non-tumor) to each patch.

**Biobank Proxy:** The CAMELYON17 dataset includes whole slide images from five pathology centers: RadboudUMC, UMCU, Erasmus MC, UMCG and the Institute Jules Bordet. To create a realistic federated setup, the dataset was partitioned among 5 clients based on the hospitals from which they were sourced.

**Data Harmonization:** Due to the inter-center variability in staining protocols, slide preparation methods, and scanning equipment in the CAMELYON17 dataset, harmonizing the visual representation of histopathology images is critical for ensuring robust model performance across sites. To address this challenge, we leveraged BeerLaNet [@xuAdaptiveStainNormalization2026], an implementation of a harmonization approach based on the Beer-Lambert law, to compute image-level frequency information for each whole-slide H&E image. This approach captures the spectral characteristics of each WSI, representing key staining features (e.g., variations in hematoxylin and eosin absorption) in a quantitative and standardized manner.

**Experimental Setup and Federated Models:** To overcome the undesirable non-biological variances and acquisition differences from each node while preserving data privacy, we utilized FedHarmony to conduct adaptive and tractable harmonization processes via the NVFlare platform. Images from different clients presented heterogeneous appearances due to differing stains. Inside each client, data were partitioned into a train (80%) and validation (20%) split following [@jiang2022harmofl].

Two federated models were evaluated. Federated Averaging (FedAvg) was used first as a baseline naive harmonization, where site-level color differences were not deliberately managed and model weights were periodically averaged across centers. Our second approach, Beer–Lambert Stain Normalization, specifically addressed stain variability. Patches were first stain-normalized to reduce inter-center variability before local models were trained and aggregated using FedAvg in NVFlare. A DenseNet convolutional neural network was leveraged for binary image classification. The network accepted RGB image patches with an input resolution of 96 × 96 pixels (3 × 96 × 96) and produced predictions over the two output classes.

## 2.4 Omnigenome: Federated Pangenome Graph Construction

In the service of constructing a federated pangenome graph, we used de novo assembled FASTA data from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC) [@Liao2023-gj]. As construction of a pangenome across all chromosomes would be computationally intractable on the timescale of a three-day hackathon, we restricted our analysis to chromosomes 19 and 22. We selected these chromosomes as 22 is the smallest autosomal chromosome, and chromosome 19 is also small but additionally contains the APOE locus, which is of interest to the second aim of our project. To simulate a federated structure we split the HPRC data into 5  
chunks of 28–38 individuals, subsampled to 20 individuals per site, which could be run in parallel with initial graph construction taking approximately 2 hours for each chunk. These chunks represent simulated studies as a proof of principle for how this procedure could be completed in a federated manner.

To achieve this project, we integrated a number of tools for graph construction, manipulation, merging and refining. Firstly, within each chunk/simulated site we performed initial graph construction using the Pangenome Graph Builder (PGGB) [@Garrison2024-wr]. This procedure created a graph for each simulated site (which we label GFA1, GFA2 ect). From these site-constructed graphs, we perform aggregation of these graphs into a megagraph using vg [@Garrison2018-bl]: firstly to convert from `.gfa` to `.vg` format, then to combine each graph using `combine -p`. We refer to this combined graph as a MegaGraph. We envisage that the aggregation step would occur at the orchestration level of an NVIDIA FLARE (NVFlare) implementation, allowing information to be leveraged across multiple studies (e.g., UK Biobank, All of Us) without exposing individual-level data from any study. This MegaGraph can then be deposited within each study to further refine graphs. For this stage we use minigraph [@Li2020-ww], as this pipeline allows a graph prior to seed graph construction along with de novo assembled data (FASTA files).  

![[2.4-1.png]]

         **Figure 2.4-1: High-level federated pangenome construction architecture** 

Figure 2.4-1 illustrates the overall architecture of Omnigenome's federated pangenome graph construction pipeline. In traditional pangenomics, all genome assemblies must be centralized at a single location before constructing a unified graph - a major barrier when working across institutions with sensitive patient data governed by regulations like HIPAA and GDPR.

Our approach decentralizes this process across multiple independent study sites. Each site (Study 1 through Study 4) holds its own set of genome assemblies locally. Using PGGB (PanGenome Graph Builder), each site independently constructs a local pangenome graph (Graph1, Graph2, Graph3, Graph4) in GFA (Graphical Fragment Assembly) format. Raw genomic sequences never leave the originating site, preserving data privacy and institutional sovereignty.

The local GFA graphs are then merged into a unified Mega Graph using `vg combine` from the vg toolkit. Critically, a minigraph feedback loop propagates structural variant information from the Mega Graph back to each local site. This feedback allows subsequent iterations to align new assemblies against the evolving global graph structure, progressively improving coverage and resolution of complex variation regions without requiring direct access to other sites' raw data.

The key tools powering this pipeline are PGGB for reference-unbiased local graph construction, minigraph for structural variant augmentation and feedback, and vg for graph merging and downstream analysis. This architecture is designed to be compatible with NVFlare's scatter-and-gather federated workflow pattern, where the server orchestrates the aggregation step and each client site executes local PGGB computation independently.

Figure 2.4-2 provides a detailed view of the federated pangenome construction pipeline as implemented during the hackathon, using Chromosome 19 from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC) dataset as our test case.

The pipeline begins on the left with the full Chromosome 19 FASTA data, partitioned into five non-overlapping chunks representing simulated biobank holdings: Chunk 1 (37 individuals), Chunk 2 (38 individuals), Chunk 3 (32 individuals), Chunk 4 (37 individuals), and Chunk 5 (28 individuals). ## 2.6 RAIDers: Federated Rare Disease Subtyping

This framework establishes a comprehensive methodology for characterizing molecular pathway involvement in ALS patient cohorts by integrating genetic variant data with biological annotations to enable severity-stratified profiling.

![[Figure 2.6-1.png]]
**Figure 2.6-1: Federated Rare Disease subtyping Pipeline**

### 2.6.1 Study Design and Data Sources

**Variant Database Curation**

The study foundation consists of curated pathogenic variant data from ClinVar [Landrum2013; Landrum2017; Landrum2019]. Approximately 450 variants were identified, each annotated with gene association (i.e.$SOD1$, $TARDBP$, $C9orf72$), ACMG clinical significance, molecular consequence (missense, nonsense, frameshift, and splice-site), and gnomAD population frequency data [Richards2015; VanDaele2023].

**Population Stratification Framework**

To ensure findings generalize across ancestries, cohorts were stratified using the 1000 Genomes Project superpopulations: African (AFR), Admixed American (AMR), East Asian (EAS), European (EUR), and South Asian (SAS).

### 2.6.2 Synthetic Patient Population Generation

**Probabilistic Variant Assignment Model**

The assignment of variants follows a probabilistic framework that integrates population-specific allele frequencies with pathogenicity scaling. For each patient $i$ and variant $v$, carriage is modeled as:

$$P(V_{i,v} = 1) = f_v^{(\text{pop})} \cdot \omega_v$$

Where $f_v^{(\text{pop})}$ is the superpopulation allele frequency and $\omega_v$ is the pathogenicity weight.

**Composite Severity Score Computation**

To quantify cumulative burden, the severity score $S_i$ is defined as the sum of variant products:

$$S_i = \sum_{v \in V_i} \left( \alpha_v \cdot \beta_v \cdot \gamma_v \right)$$

* **Pathogenicity Coefficient ($\alpha_v$):** Pathogenic = 1.0; Likely Pathogenic = 0.75.  
* **Molecular Consequence Weight ($\beta_v$):** Nonsense/Frameshift = 1.0; Splice-site = 0.8; Missense = 0.7.  
* **Penetrance Modifier ($\gamma_v$):** Empirically derived disease penetrance.

**Severity Category Assignment**

Patients were assigned to categories via a threshold-based piecewise function:
$$ \text{Severity Category}_i = \begin{cases} \text{Unaffected} & \text{if } S_i = 0 \\ \text{Mild} & \text{if } 0 < S_i \leq 5.5 \\ \text{Moderate} & \text{if } 5.5 < S_i \leq 8.0 \\ \text{Severe} & \text{if } S_i > 8.0 \end{cases} $$

### 2.6.3 Unsupervised Patient Stratification via Federated K-Means

**Algorithm Implementation**

Unsupervised clustering identified naturally occurring patient subgroups. A federated architecture partitioned $N$ patients into $k$ clusters by minimising the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS):

$$J = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \sum_{x_i \in C_j} | x_i - \mu_j |^2$$

Local cluster statistics were computed within each ancestral population stratum and aggregated by a central coordinator to preserve privacy. Optimal $k$ was selected by evaluating WCSS and silhouette score across $k \in {1, \ldots, 10}$, yielding $k = 5$ (silhouette score $= 0.66$). Cluster stability was confirmed via bootstrap resampling ($B = 50$), reporting the adjusted Rand index (ARI) between labels assigned on bootstrap samples and the full cohort.

**Feature Vector**

Feature vectors $x_i$ encode pathway burden scores, weighted pathway scores, binary pathway indicators, and composite scores derived from the molecular pathway annotation framework (Section 2.6.5). Patient metadata and raw genetic annotation fields — including identifiers, ancestral labels, variant consequence descriptors, and gene-level string annotations were excluded from the feature matrix.

**Cluster Assignment and Downstream Application**

The five clusters produced by federated K-Means naturally partitioned along severity score ranges. These boundaries were expressed as a deterministic mapping and applied to the full patient dataset to assign cluster labels for downstream pathway analyses:

$$\text{Cluster}_i = \begin{cases}
3 & \text{if } S_i = 0 \\
0 & \text{if } 4.0 \leq S_i \leq 5.7 \\
4 & \text{if } 6.0 \leq S_i \leq 6.6 \\
1 & \text{if } 6.9 \leq S_i \leq 7.8 \\
2 & \text{if } 8.4 \leq S_i \leq 10.0
\end{cases}$$

This yielded Cluster 3 (Unaffected), Cluster 0 (Mild, mean $S_i = 5.00$), Cluster 4 (Moderate-$ALS2$, mean $S_i = 6.32$), Cluster 1 (Moderate-$SOD1$, mean $S_i = 7.27$), and Cluster 2 (Severe, mean $S_i = 9.30$). Pathway annotation was subsequently applied independently to each patient using variant data alone, and pathway profiles were characterised within these cluster groupings (Sections 2.6.4-2.6.7).

### 2.6.4 Molecular Pathway Annotation Framework

Seven pathways were defined: Proteostasis (8 genes), RNA Metabolism (7 genes), Cytoskeletal/Axonal Transport (5 genes), Mitochondrial (6 genes), Excitotoxicity (5 genes), Vesicle Trafficking (5 genes), and DNA Damage (4 genes). A binary matrix $\mathbf{M} \in {0, 1}^{|G| [cite\_start]\times |P|}$ maps 35 genes to these pathways, accommodating pleiotropic effects.

### 2.6.5 Weighted Pathway Scoring Methodology

**Gene Evidence Weight Computation**

The gene weight $W_g$ incorporates clinical and biological factors:

$$W_g = P_g \times E_g \times C_g$$

* **Penetrance ($P_g$):** High = 1.0; Moderate = 0.7; Variable = 0.5; Risk modifier = 0.3.  
* **Evidence Multiplier ($E_g$):** Strong = 1.5; Moderate = 1.0; Limited = 0.7; Emerging = 0.5.  
* **Centrality ($C_g$):** Core hubs = 1.2–1.5; Standard = 1.0; Peripheral = 0.5–0.7.

**Interaction and Composite Scoring**

Gene-gene interaction multipliers $I$ account for non-additive effects:

$$I = \prod_{(g_1, g_2) \in G_i \times G_i, g_1 \neq g_2} m_{g_1, g_2}$$

The pathway burden score $B_{i,p}$ employs square root normalization to prevent pathway size bias:

$$B_{i,p} = \frac{\sum_{g \in G_i \cap G_p} W_g \times I}{\sqrt{N_p}}$$

The final composite score is adjusted by the number of affected pathways ($N_{\text{pathways},i}$):

$$\text{Composite Score}_i = \text{Total Burden}_i \times [1 + 0.1 \times (N_{\text{pathways},i} - 1)]$$

### 2.6.6 Statistical Analysis of Pathway Distributions

**Co-occurrence Significance and Logic**

Observed co-occurrences ($C_{p_1, p_2}$) were compared to expected counts ($E_{p_1, p_2}$) under the assumption of independence. To handle varying prevalences, the Jaccard similarity coefficient was applied:

$$J(p_1, p_2) = \frac{C_{p_1, p_2}}{n_{p_1} + n_{p_2} - C_{p_1, p_2}}$$

Statistical significance was assessed via Fisher’s exact test. The odds ratio (OR) was computed using the Haldane-Anscombe correction to prevent bias in small cells:

$$\text{OR} = \frac{(a + 0.5)(d + 0.5)}{(b + 0.5)(c + 0.5)}$$

**Group Comparisons and Effect Sizes**

Pathway involvement across clusters was tested using chi-square tests of independence. Effect sizes were quantified via Cramér’s V:

$$V = \sqrt{\frac{\chi^2}{N \times \min(r-1, c-1)}}$$

Score distribution differences across clusters were evaluated using the Kruskal-Wallis test ($H$) and epsilon squared ($\epsilon^2$) effect size. Spearman rank ($\rho_s$) and Pearson ($r$) correlations were used to assess relationships between pathway scores. To address floating-point precision in large samples, p-values were transformed as $-\log_{10}(p)$.

### 2.6.7 Network Analysis of Pathway Relationships

A pathway co-occurrence network $G = (V, E)$ was constructed. Relationships were classified into quadrants based on co-occurrence percentage and Pearson correlation:

* Dose-dependent (Cascading failure): Co-occurrence > 50%, $r > 0.5$.  
* Threshold effect (Binary disruption): Co-occurrence > 50%, $|r| [cite\_start]< 0.3$.  
* Distinct subtype (Mutually exclusive): Co-occurrence < 30%, $r < -0.3$.

### 2.6.8 Software Implementation

All computational analyses utilized Python 3.10+ with the pandas (data manipulation), numpy (numerical computation), and scipy (statistical testing) libraries. Network visualizations were implemented using D3.js. Bonferroni correction was applied for all multiple comparisons to control the false positive rate.

## 2.7 OncoLearn: Federated Cancer Subtyping with Multi-Omic Data

The OncoLearn framework is designed to address the challenges of data privacy and high dimensionality in cancer genomics. We utilize a distributed architecture to classify breast cancer (BRCA) into five molecular subtypes: Basal, Her2, LumA, LumB, and Normal. The methodology is divided into three technical tracks: (1) A traditional machine learning and transformer based transfer learning baseline, (2) A classical federated gradient boosting approach, and (3) A multimodal transfer learning approach leveraging foundation models.

### 2.7.1 Data Source and Preprocessing

The primary data source is the TCGA-BRCA STAR TPM expression matrix accessed from the UCSC Xena Browser [@Goldman2020]. The dataset comprises 991 samples across 19,062 gene expression features. Initial preprocessing applies a $\log_2(1 + \text{TPM})$ transformation to stabilize variance. To ensure data integrity, we implement a deduplication process to retain a single sample per patient and perform rigorous alignment between clinical metadata and genomic profiles. Clinical annotations from cBioPortal [@Cerami2012-ub, @Gao2013-oz, @Cerami2012-al, @Hoadley2018-hq] are used to assign each tumor sample to one of five PAM50-like molecular subtypes which serve as the target classes for downstream classification tasks. The resulting data combination is shown in Figure 2.7-1. The data is then partitioned into a stratified 80/20 train-test split (792 training, 199 test samples) to preserve subtype distributions.

Following this common preprocessing stage, additional data preparation steps are applied depending on the modeling track used within the OncoLearn framework. Where the distinction between preprocess pipelines can be attributed to the team’s large size.

**Figure 2.7-1: EDA Visualization of Combined Datasets**

#### 2.7.1.1 ML and Transformer-Based Baseline

For the transformer-based experiments, gene expression vectors were aligned to the ordered gene list expected by the RNABert model [@pmlr-v259-gelard25a]. Missing genes were filled with zeros and vectors padded as required to match the model input dimension.

#### 2.7.1.2 Federated Classical Learning (Gradient Boosting)

For the gradient boosting experiments, the normalized expression matrix was used directly as a tabular feature matrix. Subtype labels were encoded as categorical class targets using sklearn’s StandardScaler. The training data were partitioned into multiple subsets to simulate independent institutional sites. Each partition preserved class balance through stratified sampling.

#### 2.7.1.3 Federated Multimodal Foundation Model

For the multimodal pipeline, transcriptomic expression matrices were preprocessed upstream — log-transformed and z-scored— before being passed to the RNA foundation model encoder (biomed.rna.bert.110m.mlm.multitask.v1, [@dandala2025bmfmrnaopenframeworkbuilding]). No further per-sample normalization is applied within the dataloader; values are cast to float32 at batch construction. Expression vectors were truncated to the highest-magnitude 512 features prior to encoding. When additional modalities were available, such as DICOM images from TCIA [@Clark2013-mh, @tcia-brca], sample identifiers were used to align transcriptomic features with corresponding clinical tabular and DICOM imaging records.

### 2.7.2 ML and Transformer-Based Baseline

To establish baseline performance for subtype classification, we evaluated three supervised learning approaches using the preprocessed transcriptomic dataset described in Section 2.7.1: (1) logistic regression, (2) a multilayer perceptron (MLP), and (3) a transfer learning approach using embeddings from an RNA foundation model.

For the tabular baselines, normalized gene expression vectors were used directly as input features. Logistic regression served as a linear baseline, while the MLP provided a neural network trained end-to-end to predict the five PAM50-like subtype labels.

For the transformer-based baseline, transcriptomic profiles were encoded using the BulkRNABert RNA foundation model [@pmlr-v259-gelard25a]. Gene expression vectors were mapped to the model’s ordered gene vocabulary, producing contextualized token embeddings for each gene. Embeddings were extracted from an intermediate transformer layer and aggregated by mean pooling across all gene tokens to generate a single sample-level representation. These embeddings were then used as features for downstream classification using logistic regression and random forest models..

### 2.7.3 Federated Classical Learning (Gradient Boosting)

This track implements a distributed federated learning pipeline using XGBoost under the NVIDIA FLARE (NVFlare) framework. Multiple simulated institutional sites collaboratively train a gradient-boosted decision tree model while maintaining local data custody on isolated AWS EC2 instances. The federated job uses NVFlare’s ScatterAndGather workflow across 100 communication rounds, where a central server aggregates client contributions to maintain the global model.

The training process follows a tree-level bagging strategy implemented through NVFlare’s XGBBaggingAggregator. In each round, every client performs one local boosting iteration and transmits only the newly generated decision tree to the server, which aggregates trees from all participating nodes into the global ensemble. Prior to training, gene expression features are standardized using StandardScaler, missing values are imputed using median statistics, and each site performs a local 80/20 train–test split for evaluation. Local models are trained using the multi:softprob objective with histogram-based tree construction and a maximum tree depth of 8, with the number of classes inferred dynamically from the training data. Post-training, feature importance metrics—Gain, Weight, and Cover—are extracted to identify genomic features contributing most strongly to subtype classification.

### 2.7.4 Federated Multimodal Foundation Model

![[Figure2.7-2.png]]
**Figure 2.7-2: Multimodal Model Architecture**

This technical track evaluates a multimodal learning system that integrates transcriptomic expression data with clinical tabular features and DICOM-derived imaging (Figure 2.7-2). Transcriptomic inputs are encoded using the biomed.rna.bert.110m.mlm.multitask.v1 RNA foundation model [@dandala2025bmfmrnaopenframeworkbuilding]. Gene expression vectors are passed directly to the transformer encoder, which produces a fixed-dimensional embedding for each sample.

The resulting transcriptomic embeddings are combined with representations derived from the clinical and imaging encoders using a gated late-fusion mechanism. Training is performed within a federated setting in which only the gene encoder weights are synchronized across participating sites, while the clinical and imaging encoders remain local to each institution. This design enables collaborative training of the transcriptomic representation while preserving data locality for additional modalities. Model performance is evaluated using the held-out test data and summarized through confusion matrices and comparative analyses across modeling approaches.

### 2.7.4 Privacy Governance

The architecture ensures privacy compliance by employing NVFlare’s privacy-preserving filters. No raw transcriptomic or imaging data ever leaves the local institutional sites. Only encoded embeddings, tree structures, and evaluation metrics like classification accuracy and multi-class ROC-AUC gets communicated. The entire workflow is Dockerized to ensure hardware-level reproducibility across various GPU environments, using the $uv$ and $renv$ as package managers to maintain a consistent Python 3.12 and R 4.0+ environment across all federated nodes.

## 2.8 PRSAggregator: Federated Polygenic Risk Score Harmonization

### 2.8.1 Data Sources and PGS Parsing

Polygenic risk score (PGS) scoring files were obtained from the PGS Catalog[@lambert_2021] and spanned multiple ancestries, including European, African, East Asian, South Asian, and Hispanic/Latino populations. The selected scores focused primarily on type 2 diabetes–related traits. The scoring files exhibited substantial heterogeneity with respect to genome builds, weight types (including beta coefficients, odds ratios, and log-odds), and annotation completeness.

Each scoring file was parsed to extract structured metadata, including PGS identifier, reported ancestry, associated trait, genome build, and weight type. Variant-level information was also extracted, including rsID, effect and non-effect alleles, and reported genomic positions. Harmonized genomic coordinates provided by the PGS Catalog (hm_chr and hm_pos) were used when available.

To ensure cross-study comparability, all variants were mapped to a canonical locus identifier defined by their harmonized chromosomal position, expressed as locus_id = hm_chr:hm_pos. Effect sizes were converted to a common numeric representation and normalized to produce a unified weight_scaled variable. This harmonization enabled integration of heterogeneous scoring files into a shared analytical framework.

### 2.8.2 Variant Harmonization and Annotation

Variants were harmonized across genome builds using the harmonized coordinates supplied by the PGS Catalog. Each unique locus was subsequently annotated using the Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (VEP). This annotation process yielded biologically meaningful descriptors, including associated gene(s), gene region classification (such as exonic, intronic, or intergenic), mutation type (for example missense, synonymous, or splice-site), most severe predicted consequence, and the number of affected genes.

By incorporating functional annotations, loci were transformed from purely numerical coordinates into biologically interpretable units, facilitating downstream interpretation of embedding structure and cross-ancestry behavior.

### 2.8.3 Sparse Feature Matrix Construction

A sparse matrix representation was constructed in which rows corresponded to PGSs and columns corresponded to harmonized loci. Matrix entries contained normalized effect sizes (weight_scaled). Given the high dimensionality and sparsity inherent in genome-wide data, the matrix was optionally restricted to the top-K highest-magnitude variants per PGS to maintain computational feasibility on standard hardware.

The resulting matrix was stored in compressed sparse row (CSR) format. Associated metadata explicitly documented PGS identities, locus indices, and feature schemas to ensure reproducibility and interpretability.

### 2.8.4 PGS-Level Representation Learning

To derive compact representations of polygenic risk scores, dimensionality reduction techniques were applied to the PGS × locus matrix. Both singular value decomposition and autoencoder-based models were explored. Models were trained using explicit train/test splits and evaluated using reconstruction loss and cosine distance metrics in the original feature space.

Each PGS was mapped to a low-dimensional latent vector intended to capture ancestry-specific genetic architecture, disease-related signal, and overlap structure across scores.

### 2.8.5 Variant-Level Representation Learning

To investigate locus-level structure, the matrix representation was transposed such that each variant was represented as a vector of its effects across all PGSs. Formally, each variant i was represented as Variant_i = [w_i1, w_i2, …, w_iB], where w_ij denotes the effect size of variant i within PGS j.

An autoencoder model was trained on these variant-level vectors to produce low-dimensional embeddings summarizing how each locus behaves across multiple polygenic scores. Variants were optionally filtered by minimum support criteria, requiring presence in multiple PGSs to emphasize cross-cohort signals.

### 2.8.6 Cross-Ancestry Sharing Analysis

For each locus, the number of distinct ancestries exhibiting a non-zero effect was computed. Variants were categorized according to the degree of cross-ancestry sharing, ranging from ancestry-specific loci to variants shared across multiple ancestries. Primary ancestry and primary trait labels were assigned based on the ancestry or trait contributing the largest summed absolute effect for that locus.

This stratification enabled systematic analysis of ancestry robustness and cohort-specific genetic effects.

### 2.8.7 Visualization and Interpretation

Learned embeddings were visualized using principal component analysis (PCA) and uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP). PGS embeddings were colored by ancestry and trait, while variant embeddings were colored according to primary ancestry, primary trait, and cross-ancestry sharing category. These visualizations were used to examine relationships among PGSs and variants in the learned embedding space.

## 2.9 MuFFLe: Multimodal Federated Fusion Learning

We developed two example workflows to demonstrate the capabilities of MuFFLe for federated learning: (1) creating interpretable models for federated workflows and (2) enabling federated learning across institutions through shared embedding rather than the traditional sharing of model weights.

### Workflow 1: Simulating Federation Across Differently Resourced Institutions

To model the performance of federated learning with unequal data size and quality, due to unequal access to healthcare resources, we simulate two differently resourced hospitals. Site 1, with more patients and multimodal data (clinical and RNA-seq), and Site 2, the low-resourced site, with fewer patients and exclusively clinical data. Using federated learning, we train a multimodal model to predict tumor progression (binary outcome: 1 if progression occurs, 0 otherwise).

We used data from the CHIMERA Challenge [@chimera_miccai2025_workshop], specifically Task 3 (clinical and RNA-seq data). Task 3 contains 176 patients (126 in Cohort A, 50 in Cohort B), with 19,359 RNA-seq gene features and 15 clinical features. RNA-seq data from Cohorts A and B were generated using different protocols, simulating heterogeneity across institutions. No batch correction was applied, and clinical data varied between cohorts.

Site 1 was assigned 100 patients from Cohort A and Site 2 assigned all 50 patients from Cohort B. The remaining 26 patients from Cohort 1 were held out as a test set. As stated above, to model the inequality between the two hospitals, Site 2 comprised clinical data, while Site 1 also included RNA-seq data. Federated learning was implemented using NVFlare’s Client-Server model, assigning each site independent hyperparameters (e.g., smaller batch size, higher learning rate for Site 2 due to fewer data points). Both sites performed supervised fine-tuning on local data and shared updates with the server, which aggregated these using NVFlare’s FedAvg algorithm [@Roth2023]. The global model was iteratively updated and evaluated on the held-out test set. Further details and code are available in MuFFLe’s GitHub repository (See Data and Software Availability).

### Workflow 2: Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) for Multimodal Data Fusion

We implemented Vertical Federated Learning (VFL) to enable collaborative training across institutions that share patient identifiers but hold different data modalities. In this setup, each client locally encodes its data (e.g., imaging or RNA-seq) using a modality-specific model and transmits intermediate embeddings to a central server. The server performs multimodal fusion and downstream predictions, preserving data privacy while learning a joint representation.

To robustly handle heterogeneous and partially observed modalities, the server model incorporates a gated fusion mechanism combined with attention. The gating module learns modality-specific importance weights, allowing the model to dynamically modulate the contribution of each client based on feature relevance and availability. In parallel, an attention mechanism enables adaptive interaction across modality embeddings, improving representational alignment and mitigating dominance by any single modality. This design naturally supports scenarios where some samples may lack one or more modalities, as the fusion process is learned rather than hard-coded.

The framework is evaluated on synthetically generated (dummy) multimodal data to validate correctness, stability, and extensibility of the vertical federated learning setup. However, the architecture is directly extensible to real-world biomedical datasets such as *CHIMERA* without modification to the federated protocol. Furthermore, we explore an alternative pipeline in which the image encoder is replaced with a Vision Transformer (ViT)-based foundation model, treating the image backbone as a large pretrained model while preserving the same vertical federated fusion strategy. This demonstrates the flexibility of the proposed VFL framework to integrate both conventional deep learning models and large-scale foundation models within a unified, privacy-preserving multimodal learning paradigm.

We use a late fusion architecture with modality-specific encoders: an RNA encoder projects gene expression data into 256-dim embeddings, while a clinical encoder maps patient features to 64-dim embeddings. These are concatenated and fed through a risk prediction head. Missing modalities are handled by substituting zero embeddings. Training uses NVFlare's FedAvg algorithm across simulated sites, where each site specializes in one modality (e.g., Site-1 trains on clinical data, Site-2 on RNA). Sites receive the global model, train locally, and send weight updates back for aggregation—enabling collaborative learning while preserving privacy.

2.10 FedProFit: Federated Prediction of Deep Mutational Scanning

FedProFit is built using a Hydra architecture [@Yadan2019Hydra] that combines a frozen pre-trained protein language model backbone with locally trainable prediction heads, implemented using NVIDIA FLARE [@Roth2022] for federated learning orchestration. We evaluate our framework on the ProteinGym Substitution Benchmark [@Notin2023], which comprises approximately 2.4 million missense variants across 217 DMS assays partitioned into four federated clients based on biological domain (Human, Virus, Prokaryote, and Eukaryote) (Figure 2.10-1).

![[Figure 2.10-1.png]]
**Figure 2.10-1: Distribution of species and proteins across different taxa (Human, Virus, Prokaryote, Eukaryote) in the ProteinGym benchmark**

The system architecture consists of a pre-trained BioNeMo model [@BioNeMo2024] (e.g., ESM-2 or MegaMolBART) that serves as a frozen feature extractor, taking amino acid sequences as input and producing protein embeddings. On top of this frozen backbone, each federated client adds a lightweight regression head locally, consisting of a pooling layer that aggregates sequence embeddings and a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) that predicts DMS scores. The prediction head is trainable and added locally to each client, allowing for local adaptation while keeping the computationally expensive backbone frozen. During federated training, we employ the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm [@McMahan2017] where the central server initializes prediction head weights and distributes them to all clients. Each client then trains the prediction head on their local data using Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss optimized with AdamW, after which only the updated prediction head weights are sent back to the server for weighted averaging. This process repeats for multiple communication rounds, ensuring that only lightweight prediction head weights are transmitted, significantly reducing communication overhead compared to training the full model.

To use FedProFit, institutions first partition their DMS datasets by biological domain and set up federated client nodes, with each client corresponding to a specific taxon (Table 2.10-1). The BioNeMo backbone [@BioNeMo2024] is pre-loaded on all clients, and the central server initializes and distributes the prediction head weights. Each client then trains the prediction head locally on their domain-specific data for a specified number of epochs, using the frozen BioNeMo model to extract features from protein sequences. After local training, clients send only their updated prediction head weights to the central server, which performs weighted averaging based on the number of training samples per client. The aggregated global model is then distributed back to all clients for the next round of federated training. This process continues for multiple communication rounds until convergence, enabling collaborative model improvement while preserving data privacy. The resulting federated model can then be used to predict DMS scores for new protein variants, with each client benefiting from the collective knowledge learned across all participating institutions.

## 3.1 FedGen: Federated Learning Infrastructure & Synthetic Genomic Data

We successfully developed and deployed an end-to-end federated learning infrastructure for genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and meta-analysis that demonstrates the feasibility of privacy-preserving genomic analysis at biobank scale.

### Synthetic Data Generation with Realistic Heterogeneity

We generated 10 heterogeneous synthetic datasets representing geographically distributed biobanks or healthcare institutions. Each site contained around 100k individuals with around 500k genotyped SNPs, totaling approximately 1 million samples and 5 million SNPs across all sites. The data mimicked realistic features of multi-site genomic studies, i.e., population-level heterogeneity with variable sample sizes and SNP densities across sites mirrored real-world differences in data collection and genotyping platforms; A preserved linkage disequilibrium (LD) structure wherein LDAK-generated genotypes maintained realistic LD patterns needed for accurate association testing; with biologically plausible phenotypes representative of a complex trait such as Parkinson's disease with a case-control status with 1% prevalence, heritability $h^2=0.25$, and 20 causal variants per site; and finally a covariate structure accounting for age and sex explaining 10% of phenotypic variance, capturing typical demographic confounding.  This synthetic data framework provides a reproducible testbed for federated genomic analysis that can be easily modified to simulate different disease traits, sample sizes, or genetic architectures.

### Site-Level GWAS Analysis with Dockerized REGENIE

Each of the 10 sites independently performed a GWAS using a containerized REGENIE workflow. The implementation using Docker containers ensured consistent results across heterogeneous computing environments (local workstations, cloud instances, HPC facilities);
Scalability where workflows successfully handled biobank-scale data (~100K samples with 500K SNPs) on standard compute resources provided by NVIDIA Brev GPU instances; and reproducibility with identical container images across clients guaranteeing consistent analysis across all sites. All 10 sites successfully completed GWAS analysis and generated summary statistics suitable for meta-analysis, with no raw genotype data leaving local environments.

### Federated Meta-Analysis Using NVFlare and GWAMA

We implemented a FL framework using NVIDIA FLARE to orchestrate meta-analysis of GWAS summary statistics obtained across sites. Key infrastructure components included a central NVIDIA FLARE server deployed on AWS coordinated communication and aggregation, 10 federated clients deployed on NVIDIA Brev GPU instances representing independent data holders. Each client ran in isolated Docker containers with controlled data access

To ensure privacy-preserving aggregation of GWAS summary statistics, only summary statistics (effect sizes, standard errors, p-values) were shared between respective clients and the server. Raw genotype data remained local throughout the entire workflow with no cross-site communication. Post-GWAS meta-analysis was performed using the GWAMA software with both fixed-effects and random-effects models.

Our FL framework successfully aggregated the GWAS results across all 10 sites, wherein the meta-analysis increased the effective sample size to ~1 million individuals (Figure 3.1-1, Figure 3.1-2). Heterogeneity statistics ($I^2$ and Cochran's Q) quantified consistency of genetic effects across sites. Genome-wide significant associations were detected that would not have reached significance in single-site analyses.

Meta-analysis accounting for p-values demonstrated improved significance compared to single-site results, with several variants reaching genome-wide significance ($p < 5 \times 10^{-8}$) only in the federated analysis.

The system outputs trained global models, per-site performance metrics, validation predictions, confusion matrices, and execution logs to a structured workspace directory, with all numerical operations implementing safeguards against gradient explosion and NaN contamination through gradient clipping (clipnorm=1.0) and explicit nan_to_num conversion at data loading and aggregation steps. ## 3.9 MuFFLe: Multimodal Federated Fusion Learning

To make interpretable models for federated workflows, MuFFLe employs a late-fusion approach that incorporates trainable weights to represent the importance of each modality before concatenating them into a single, large embedding. An attention layer is then added to this single embedding. The attention weights provide insight into which features were most influential. Additionally, by examining the small linear layers in the RNA-sequencing encoder, researchers can reverse-engineer which protein-coding genes most significantly impact the prediction of tumor progression.

MuFFLe addresses the challenge of utilizing federated learning across institutions with varying healthcare capabilities because its fusion model can perform a forward pass using only clinical data or both clinical and RNAseq data. This flexibility allows institutions with missing data modalities to still update the global federated model. As healthcare capabilities improve, an institution can easily integrate additional data sources. Furthermore, institutions seeking to use novel data modalities can create a new fusion model and vertically extend the federated learning pipeline by adding a new embedding model. The model's inherent extensibility supports a continuous learning pipeline, for instance, by using an unsupervised clustering algorithm to continuously update predictions as new modalities are introduced across different institutions.

# 4. Discussion and Future Directions

## 4.1 Federated Learning in Genomic Medicine  
The developed federated learning (FL) infrastructure demonstrates a practical solution to the inherent tension between data privacy and scientific progress in genomic medicine. Historically, multi-institutional biobank datasets have been isolated due to stringent regulatory constraints (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA) and institutional review board transfer restrictions. Our framework overcomes these data silos by bringing the analysis to the data. Participating sites retain absolute sovereignty over raw genotypes and contribute to consortium-scale discoveries exclusively through the exchange of aggregate summary statistics (effect sizes, standard errors, allele frequencies, and p-values). By implementing both fixed- and random-effects models via GWAMA, the framework leverages inter-site heterogeneity to strengthen consistent association signals and identify population-specific genetic effects. This privacy-by-design approach satisfies strict data governance policies while maintaining the statistical power necessary for robust genetic discovery.

### 4.1.1 Accelerating Translational Research and Drug Discovery  
Applying this FL framework to real-world, multimodal biobank data will facilitate the identification of novel variants and pathways in underrepresented populations, improving our understanding of disease pathogenesis. Furthermore, this infrastructure addresses critical bottlenecks in pharmaceutical drug discovery. Therapeutic targets supported by human genetic evidence have a significantly higher probability of clinical approval, yet assembling sufficiently powered, diverse cohorts requires years of complex legal negotiation. Our federated approach compresses this timeline, enabling researchers to orchestrate multi-institutional target validation in weeks. The system also supports continuous integration; as new biobanks become available or existing cohorts grow, they can be seamlessly incorporated into ongoing federated analyses without requiring the reanalysis of previous sites, thereby creating a persistent and extensible research network.

### 4.1.2 Future Directions  
While the current implementation focuses on genome-wide association studies (GWAS), the architecture is readily extensible to polygenic risk score (PRS) development, gene-environment interaction modeling, and multi-omic integration.

To advance the synthetic data generation pipeline, future work will incorporate additional skewness parameters, such as severe class imbalances and diverse missingness patterns across phenotypes, genotypes, and covariates. The framework will also be expanded to support more complex phenotype generation, including repeated measurements with time-varying heritability, the inclusion of noise, and varying proportions of explained variance. Within the federated learning strategy, we plan to implement improved monitoring dashboards via TensorBoard to account for variable performance timing caused by site-specific data imbalances. Furthermore, we will explore alternative learning strategies utilizing different GWAS software and integrate rigorous validation frameworks, such as leave-one-site-out cross-validation. Finally, to improve meta-analysis and aggregation, future iterations will develop robust compatibility blocks to harmonize disparate outputs from varying local algorithms. This will enable iterative meta-analysis techniques that seamlessly integrate cross-validation learning to continuously refine the global model.

## 4.2 FedPathHarmony: Domain Harmonization for Federated Histopathology

Performance was similar for both FedAvg (AUCROC: 0.9977) and our approach, FedPathHarmony (AUCROC: 0.9978). In our current design, we are utilizing BeerLaNet to harmonize our images, at each client, but this only addresses intra-biobank variability, not inter-biobank variability. As such, FedPathHarmony did not provide the improvement that was expected. In a future work we will explore a federated framework that facilitates communication of local stain variability to a global server, allowing for a globally averaged colorspace that can be communicated back to the clients as a pre-processing normalization step. The globally averaged colorspace is inherently privacy-preserving, as feature-level information is not preserved during federation.

The CAMELYON17 is a valuable starting point, but we need to test FedPathHarmony on other cancer types, staining methods, and through larger hospital networks to establish its generalizability. To achieve this, Biobank data access would be highly beneficial. Using biobanks’ multi-institutional cohorts, which contain data on different cancer types and associated patient outcomes, will help us develop federated AI models for precision oncology. This will be especially helpful in cases of rare cancer subtypes where individual institutions usually lack sufficient cases for robust model training. Federated approaches like ours can help achieve the performance of global models while enabling collaboration across biobanks that can otherwise not share sensitive data due to regulatory constraints.

## 4.3 FedViz: Auditing Federated Readiness Across Biobank Consortia

### Toward Automated Interoperability

The baseline Readiness Index of 1.04% identified by FedViz: Auditing Federated Readiness Across Biobank Consortia is a call to action for the biomedical research community. It highlights that even in massive consortia like IHCC, manual harmonization is a failing strategy for the age of Big Data. Our results demonstrate that while federated learning can produce high-performing global models (84.13% accuracy), these models are currently restricted to a tiny sliver of the actual data collected. This "feature scarcity" risks over-indexing on demographics and vitals while leaving specialized disease-specific findings in institutional silos.

### 4.3.1 Overcoming Silos with Semantic Intelligence

The next phase of FedViz: Auditing Federated Readiness Across Biobank Consortia focuses on bridging this gap through Automated Semantic Mapping. We are currently integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) to scan unharmonized variables and suggest "Synonym Matches" across site terminologies (e.g., mapping "Nicotine Dependence" in one site to "Tobacco Use History" in another). By automating this synonym discovery, we project an increase in the Readiness Index from 1.04% to over 5%, effectively quadrupling the feature space available for federated training without requiring manual site-level intervention.

### 4.3.2 Enhancing Visualization for Multi-Site Evaluation

Future work will also introduce "Site Density" comparisons and "Multimodal Distribution" graphs. These will allow researchers to see not just if a variable is present, but how its distribution varies across sites, helping to detect domain shifts early in the pipeline. We also plan to implement "Matching Modes" that allow researchers to toggle between Exact, Domain-Expanded, and Hybrid views of variable co-occurrence, improving the ability to find strong candidates for new harmonized variable definitions.

### 4.3.3 Real-World Application and Biobank Access

Access to real-world biobank data would allow us to take advantage of this framework to identify potentially new variants and pathways in underserved populations with more robustness. We envision FedViz: Auditing Federated Readiness Across Biobank Consortia as an essential "Auditing Layer" between raw biobank heterogeneity and robust federated training. By choosing an MIT License, we enable any biobank to deploy this tool locally, allowing them to audit their internal readiness and prepare their data for the global federated ecosystem while maintaining complete data sovereignty and trust.

## 4.4 Omnigenome: Federated Pangenome Graph Construction

These results demonstrate that high-quality pangenome graphs can be constructed in a federated setting without direct access to centralized raw genomic data. The close correspondence between our chromosome 19 graphs and the HPRC reference pangenomes suggests that locally constructed graphs, when generated using standardized pipelines such as PGGB, can faithfully capture large-scale structural features of population-scale references. Importantly, the ability to flexibly subsample individuals and construct cohort-specific graphs highlights a practical path toward collaborative pangenomics across institutions with heterogeneous data access constraints, addressing a major bottleneck in scaling pangenome approaches beyond centralized consortia.

The implementation of a generalized mini-graph extraction framework further extends the utility of federated pangenomes by enabling locus-specific analysis. By focusing on localized genomic regions, mini-graphs offer a compact representation of haplotypic structure that is particularly well-suited for downstream association studies, where global genome-wide graphs may be unnecessarily large or computationally burdensome. This localized perspective is especially relevant for interpreting disease-associated loci, such as APOE, where genomic background and structural context may modulate risk beyond single-variant effects captured by traditional GWAS approaches.

Looking forward, mini-graph–based uniqueness hashing presents an intriguing direction for privacy-preserving genomic data sharing. Rather than exchanging raw sequences or individual-level genotypes, biobanks could share anonymized hashes derived from local pangenome structure, enabling cross-cohort comparisons while respecting data sovereignty and regulatory constraints. Future work will need to assess the stability, sensitivity, and interpretability of such hashes across diverse populations and sequencing technologies, as well as their utility in large-scale association and prediction tasks. Nonetheless, our results suggest that federated pangenome construction coupled with localized graph representations may provide a scalable and privacy-aware foundation for next-generation population genomics.

## 4.5 Med_SNP_Deconvolution: Privacy-Preserving Ancestry Inference

Our results demonstrate that recombination-defined haploblock clusters provide privacy-enhanced genomic representation with high predictive performance and substantially reduced re-identification risk. The framework achieves dimensionality reduction from millions of SNPs to thousands of haploblocks while maintaining biological interpretability through alignment with evolutionarily coherent recombination boundaries [@Halldorsson2019; Palsson2025]. XGBoost and deep learning architectures both effectively capture population structure, with XGBoost offering interpretability, while deep learning modeling long-range interactions for fine-scale ancestry deconvolution. Federated learning via NVIDIA FLARE [@Roth2022] enables multi-institutional collaboration with more than 0.80 of AUROC degradation compared to centralized training (0.75). However, key limitations include restriction to chromosome 6 and three continental populations, which may potentially inflate performance due to MHC diversity, loss of rare variant information critical for Mendelian disease analysis [Bamshad2019], lack of formal differential privacy guarantees [@Dwork2014], and potential systematic errors from population-mismatched recombination maps [@Baudat2010].

Future work should extend validation to genome-wide analysis across all 1000 Genomes populations and deploy across diverse biobank infrastructures—from public research cohorts (UK Biobank, All of Us) to institutional biobanks (Million Veteran Program, Biobank Japan, Geisinger MyCode) and private healthcare systems—enabling systematic evaluation under real-world conditions with heterogeneous platforms, diverse ancestries, and varied governance policies. Beyond scaling to more data sources, critical research directions include Byzantine-robust federated learning to handle malicious or compromised nodes that may inject poisoned model updates, contribute fabricated gradients, or attempt model inversion attacks to extract sensitive genomic information from other participants. As federation scales to dozens or hundreds of nodes, additional challenges emerge: heterogeneous computational capabilities requiring adaptive aggregation strategies, communication efficiency under bandwidth constraints, asynchronous participation patterns, and dynamic membership where nodes join or leave during training. Incorporating differential privacy guarantees, secure aggregation protocols, and anomaly detection mechanisms for identifying adversarial behavior becomes essential for clinical deployment. Furthermore, implementing population-specific recombination maps, data-driven haploblock boundary learning, and establishing governance frameworks with clear accountability for federated clinical genomics will be crucial. As genomic medicine integrates into routine healthcare, frameworks like SNP Deconvolution provide a pathway to realize collaborative data science benefits while maintaining robustness against adversarial threats, respecting patient privacy, and advancing health equity in precision medicine.

## 4.6 RAIDers: Federated Rare Disease Subtyping

The current study represents a proof-of-concept framework for molecular subtyping in ALS, and several important extensions are needed to advance this work toward clinical utility. All analyses were conducted on synthetic patient data derived from population-level variant frequencies, and findings must be validated using real genomic data. We also acknowledge that several aspects of the current pipeline may not capture the full complexity of ALS. As the framework matures, more sophisticated modeling strategies, such as graph-based pathway representation or Bayesian variant weighting frameworks, may be explored in future iterations.

Beyond genomic validation, our pipeline is one-dimensional, and incorporating complementary omics data layers would provide a more complete molecular portrait of ALS patients and better reflect the heterogeneity of the disease. As the pipeline is refined, there is also potential to extend its applicability to other rare diseases. Lastly, extending the federated architecture to utilize clinical data from different sites will also be necessary to address the data accessibility challenges for rare-disease research.

## 4.7 OncoLearn: Federated Cancer Subtyping with Multi-Omic Data

Future work could focus on expanding the OncoLearn framework to support additional datasets and improve reproducibility and usability across modeling pipelines. Planned extensions that we did not have time for include integrating CPTAC data into the CLI and data-loading workflow and exposing standardized dataloaders for each dataset source (e.g., TCGA, TCIA, CPTAC) and modality (e.g., imaging, tabular), enabling users to clone the repository and begin modeling with minimal setup. Ongoing efforts aim to fully integrate the separate team implementations into the unified OncoLearn library, with testing already underway for the multimodal pipeline. Future work could also evaluate the portability of the Docker-based development environment across heterogeneous systems and explore alternative orchestration platforms such as Kubernetes. Additional directions include enforcing consistent train–validation–test splits across all modeling tracks, implementing strategies to address missing modalities through imputation, and extending the framework beyond BRCA to additional cancer types. Finally, additional work could incorporate standardized visualization tools and feature importance methods to support biomarker discovery and develop large-scale pipelines spanning multiple cancer types to explore cross-cancer patterns in molecular signatures.

## 4.8 PRSAggregator: Federated Polygenic Risk Score Harmonization

This work demonstrates that structural heterogeneity across PGS is substantial even within a single phenotype, and that explicit profiling should precede any aggregation or downstream modeling. In the Type 2 diabetes example, the PRS profiling module quantified large differences in SNP and gene counts and revealed limited overlap across scores via UpSet plots, clarifying where redundancy versus complementarity is likely to arise. The PRS Locus Viewer then operationalizes “deep dives” into specific genomic regions by allowing direct comparison of effect weights, gene proximity, and locus-level context, which helps validate whether apparent overlaps are biologically coherent or artifact-driven. Finally, the federated representation learning framework provides an interpretable, privacy-preserving way to compare PRSs by treating each score as a client and learning PRS and variant level data that separate ancestry specific structure from admixed ancestry. Together, these modules form a coherent workflow that supports principled PRS selection and aggregation decisions using only harmonized PGS and variant level information, without requiring access to individual level genotypes.

Future work should scale the framework beyond a small set of T2D scores to broader disease areas and a larger, more diverse set of ancestries and traits, enabling more robust conclusions about cross-phenotype and cross-population sharing. Methodologically, harmonization can be strengthened by incorporating stricter allele alignment and quality controls, LD-aware locus grouping when appropriate, and expanded functional annotations (e.g., regulatory elements, eQTL links, pathway context) to improve interpretability of variant embeddings. A key next step is to connect “structural similarity” to “predictive behavior” by benchmarking whether embedding proximity and overlap metrics correlate with external performance measures, calibration, and generalizability, including evaluation of ensemble and aggregation strategies informed by these representations. From a federated learning perspective, the client-like abstraction can be extended to institution or biobank level deployments where sharing is restricted, using privacy preserving summaries and standardized outputs to enable cross site comparison without centralizing sensitive data. The locus viewer and profiling pipeline can evolve into a more automated, end-to-end toolkit with reproducible configuration, phenotype centric organization, and a web enabled interface that reduces manual file handling while preserving local execution options for restricted environments.

## 4.9 MuFFLe: Multimodal Federated Fusion Learning

Our proof-of-concept demonstrates that federated learning can enable multimodal cancer prognosis modeling across institutions with heterogeneous data availability. However, several enhancements could significantly improve clinical utility. First, the current late fusion approach treats all modalities equally during concatenation. Incorporating attention mechanisms or per-modality weights for importance would provide interpretability into which data sources drive predictions for individual patient data points. Interpretability is crucial for clinical adoption as oncologists need to understand model reasoning in order to use the results to supplement their diagnosis.

Second, the CHIMERA dataset’s ~200 patients, while sufficient for a proof-of-concept, limits rigorous evaluation of model performance. Expanding to larger cohorts and additional cancer types would validate generalizability. Our current implementation also excludes histopathology imaging to reduce scope, so integrating new imaging features through pretrained pathology foundation models is a great potential extension that would improve prognostic accuracy.

The modular architecture readily accommodates additional encoder branches, and the federated environment enables institutions with specialized imaging capabilities to contribute.

The federated paradigm most importantly opens up opportunities for continuous model improvement as more institutions and their data join the network. Unlike centralized models which are static, federated systems can incorporate new data sources over time without requiring the re-collection of old data. Future directions could include implementing more privacy considerations, adjustments for personalized federated learning approaches that adapt the global model to site-specific patient populations, and cancer hopefully validation on prospective clinical cohorts. We envision MuFFLe as a foundation for privacy-preserving precision oncology, enabling collaborative research across institutional boundaries while maintaining trust and regulatory compliance.

## 4.10 FedProFit: Federated Prediction of Deep Mutational Scanning

Our FedProFit framework addresses critical challenges in collaborative protein fitness prediction by enabling privacy-preserving model training across distributed datasets. The use of frozen pre-trained protein language models as feature extractors provides a practical solution that balances performance, computational efficiency, and communication overhead. The federated learning approach is particularly well-suited for the protein engineering domain, where data is often siloed across institutions due to proprietary interests, regulatory constraints, or competitive advantages. By allowing multiple institutions to collaboratively improve a shared model without sharing raw sequence data, FedProFit enables the scientific community to leverage diverse biological datasets while respecting data privacy and ownership.

The partitioning of data by biological domain (Human, Virus, Prokaryote, Eukaryote) creates a realistic non-IID (non-independent and identically distributed) scenario that is common in federated learning applications. This heterogeneity tests the robustness of the FedAvg algorithm and may reveal opportunities for domain-specific adaptations or personalized federated learning approaches. The Hydra architecture, with its frozen backbone and locally trainable heads, offers an efficient compromise between model personalization and communication efficiency, making it particularly suitable for cross-institutional collaboration where computational resources and network bandwidth may be limited.

Several directions for future work are promising. First, systematic hyperparameter optimization for the regression MLP architecture, including layer depth, hidden dimensions, dropout rates, and activation functions, could improve predictive performance. Second, exploration of advanced federated learning algorithms beyond FedAvg, such as FedProx [@Li2020] or SCAFFOLD [@Karimireddy2020], may better handle the non-IID data distribution across biological domains and improve convergence rates. Third, extending the framework to multi-task learning, simultaneously predicting both continuous DMS scores and binary fitness classifications, could improve generalization and provide more comprehensive fitness assessments. Additionally, real-world deployment with actual distributed institutions would validate the framework's practicality, addressing challenges such as network latency, client availability, and data quality heterogeneity. Finally, incorporating interpretability methods such as attention visualization or gradient-based techniques could provide biological insights by identifying which sequence regions contribute most to fitness predictions, enhancing both scientific understanding and model trustworthiness.

### Biobank Data Integration

Each participating team aims to deploy FedProFit within their secure infrastructures to train on proprietary biobanks and genomic repositories—ranging from human clinical variant databases to viral surveillance archives—without ever exposing sensitive raw sequences. This enables institutions to validate fitness predictions against real-world phenotypic data held in strict privacy, effectively bridging the gap between computational scoring and clinical or epidemiological observations.
